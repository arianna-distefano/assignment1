Chosen Web Experience: Frank Gehry and the Walt Disney Concert Hall

When first opening the experience, what I initially paid attention to was the orchestral music. Which I then found out was Dvořák's Symphony No. 7 in D minor through hovering over the “Sound On” button. As the user spends more time on the site, the music transitions to other orchestral pieces. This set the overall tone for the experience as well as created the foundation for further immersion within the website.
The discrete actions I performed mostly consisted of scrolling, as it was explicitly directed by the experience that the user had to scroll in order to progress in the story. Other actions, such as clicking and mouse hovering, were also present, as well as reading.
I found that the section that I spent the most time interacting with was the video portion, where the user is put through a series of videos that explain the different segments of the story that the experience is telling.
The most common action that I performed during the experience was scrolling, as it was instructed by the experience when they first opened the webpage. The scrolling allowed users to further the experience at their own pace, which included multiple drone shots that were controlled by the extent of how fast or slow the user chose to scroll. Scrolling also resulted in various text changes, including changes to the size of text portions and to which sections of text appeared and disappeared.
The intended primary goal of the experience was to narrate the process where Frank Gehry designed the Walt Disney Concert Hall. The story journeyed through different stages of the design process and how the final design came to be. The experience encouraged users to flow through the narrative chronologically in order to get the full story. However, the web page did contain a menu section where each segment can be viewed freely. Nevertheless, the users were still encouraged to consume the story in chronological order as each heading was labelled with a chapter number.
The simple navigation of the website left little room for error and discouraged users from skipping ahead, ultimately disrupting the chronological sequence that the experience was made to be enjoyed in. I found this most prevalent in the video sections where users were notified that they were “leaving the interactive story” and invited to resume by clicking or scrolling back up.
I found that the experience was designed to be interacted with over a longer duration of time, for around one time. Due to the nature of the experience, users were encouraged to spend a considerable amount of time viewing each interactive story and reading each chapter. The webpage contained its own inbuilt orchestral playlist, as well as taking the user back to the landing page every time the page is reloaded or reopened. This further influenced me to think that the experience was meant to be enjoyed in one extended sitting rather than short repeated visits. Assuming that users take the time to watch each interactive story in full and read through each text segment, the time could easily extend over 15 minutes.
The experience references different orchestral pieces throughout the entirety of the time the user spends on the website (assuming they chose to keep the music on), as well as composers and musical figures. Different architectural models and diagrams are also present across the entirety of the experience. The structure of the interactive story segments were reminiscent of short films in the way they were produced and placed at the beginning of each chapter. I found that the overall format of the website was similar in the way curated museum exhibits or art galleries were displayed, mainly in the way that each chapter of the experience contained different images and pieces that were accompanied by captions and descriptions.
This design guided the user to carefully take their time and view each section of the narrative. Rather than pushing to meet an end goal, the user was invited to appreciate the details of each step of the design process. This structure evoked feelings of curiosity and intrigue for each stage, potentially inspiring users to do their own research about the architecture of other buildings.
I found the experience quite enjoyable overall, however, during some parts of the interactive video, I felt that they went on for too long and felt a bit bored during the videos that I didn’t find as interesting. However, the most satisfying part of the experience was the overall duration and storytelling. The nature of the experience allowed the information to be easily digestible and I wasn't left thinking “how much more of this is there?” or “this is getting really boring”.
